Chalk

Entry 005 · 2026-03-03

I didn’t expect the pavement to say anything.

It was just a school lot — Northwest — cracked concrete, faint lines, the residue of buses and rain and years of foot traffic. The kind of place nobody photographs on purpose. The kind of place you pass through on the way to something else. And yet, scattered across the ground were drawings — chalk, paint, symbols, half-erased gestures — left behind by students who likely didn’t think of themselves as philosophers or historians or citizens of an experiment begun centuries ago.

But they were.

Because John Adams’ vision only works if most people don’t know they’re inside it.

Adams wrote that he must study politics and war so his sons could study mathematics and philosophy, so their sons could study painting, poetry, and music. It wasn’t a ladder to power. It was a handoff of attention. A belief that if enough stability were secured upstream, downstream generations could finally ask quieter questions. Not How do we survive? but What do we make? What do we notice? What do we feel like saying?

Standing there, looking down at that pavement, it landed: this is the yield.

Not marble halls or grand speeches, but anonymous marks on concrete. Kids testing symbols. Leaving color where none was required. Speaking in fragments because fragments are enough when survival isn’t the central concern. This wasn’t rebellion. It wasn’t propaganda. It wasn’t even art in the institutional sense. It was something more fragile and more honest — excess expression. Meaning spilling out because there was room for it to spill.

That’s Adams’ dream made physical.

What struck me most was how temporary it all was. Chalk washes away. Paint fades. Feet pass over it without ceremony. And still, the gesture mattered. Someone bent down. Someone chose a color. Someone decided to leave a mark knowing it wouldn’t last. That kind of action only exists in a culture where the future feels at least somewhat trustworthy.

Empires obsessed with permanence carve stone.
Secure civilizations allow chalk.

And here’s the quiet irony: while so many insist that culture is collapsing — that attention is gone, that meaning has drained out of public life — it’s still showing up exactly where Adams would have expected it to. Not online. Not in institutions. But on the ground. In places meant for transit, not reflection. In margins. In the in-between hours of a school day.

We don’t get to choose where meaning appears.
We only get to notice when it does.

That pavement wasn’t asking to be curated or archived or explained. It didn’t need a caption. It didn’t need permission. It existed because someone had enough safety, enough slack, enough inner space to say: I’m here — briefly — and this is what passed through me.

That’s the republic functioning.

Not perfectly. Not cleanly. But truthfully.

Standing there, I felt something else too — personal, but quiet. A recognition that this is the same current that runs through my own work. The same pull toward overlooked textures, residue instead of spectacle. I don’t want to shout into history. I want to leave something small and unmistakably human where someone might stumble across it later and feel less alone.

That impulse didn’t come from nowhere. It came from a world that, for all its fractures, still made space for attention. Still handed down the privilege of noticing. Still allowed a kid to draw on concrete without fearing that the world would end tomorrow.

John Adams never saw this pavement.
But he imagined it.

He imagined a day when meaning wouldn’t need to justify itself through utility or defense or dominance. When expression could be soft. Provisional. Almost accidental. When the highest achievement of a political system would be that most people forget it’s there at all.

That’s what I saw at Northwest.

Not decay — but evidence.

Evidence that the long sentence is still being written.
Evidence that the handoff worked, at least enough.
Evidence that somewhere between war and philosophy and art, we arrived at chalk on concrete — and that it was worth the cost.

The pavement was already speaking.

All I had to do was look down.
